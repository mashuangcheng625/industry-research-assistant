Executive Summary & Introduction

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The CHIPS and Science Act appropriated $50 billion to the Department of Commerce’s CHIPS for America program to support semiconductor research and development (R&D) and expand semiconductor manufacturing capacity in the United States. Within CHIPS for America, the National Institute of Standards and Technology’s (NIST) CHIPS R&D Office (CRDO) has aimed to accelerate the development and commercial deployment of foundational semiconductor technologies by establishing, connecting, and providing access to domestic research efforts, tools, resources, workers, and facilities. A key element to achieving these CHIPS R&D goals is to accelerate the private sector-led development and deployment by industry of effective technical standards. CHIPS R&D has developed a comprehensive standards roadmap that aligns with the needs of private sector semiconductor standards efforts, the requirements of CHIPS legislation, and the provisions of the U.S. Government National Standards Strategy for Critical and Emerging Technology1 . The CHIPS R&D standards roadmap is centered on a vision for a vibrant microelectronics standards ecosystem that is smarter, faster, and more inclusive and agile in enabling innovation. CHIPS R&D has undertaken a series of events to solicit the global semiconductor industry’s perspectives on, and input into, aligning the government effort with the industry’s standards needs. The first of these CHIPS R&D standards activities was a Standards Summit event held in September 2023, in Washington, D.C., which brought together private sector thought leaders to identify strategic standards priorities and recommendations for the semiconductor sector2 . One recommendation that emerged from the Summit was to explore the concept of standards readiness level metrics. The concept of standards readiness level was described as analogous to existing metrics such as technology readiness levels (TRLs)3 , which help assess a technology's maturity from basic research to commercial adoption. Participants suggested a community-driven effort to establish consensus on standards readiness metrics that would facilitate strategic planning and road mapping and help align pre-standards research with standards development. Additional components of a standards readiness level framework could include metrics to help assess if emerging technologies are ready for standardization. As a result of this community recommendation, CHIPS R&D held a Standardization Readiness Level Workshop (from here, ‘workshop’). This workshop brought together technical experts within the standards community to consider perspectives on metrics for standardization readiness that could inform the semiconductor industry’s standards strategies and activities. The workshop discussions focused on exploring the development of two new tools: a standardization readiness framework and a standards readiness level scale (StRL). These tools could be used to guide standardization efforts in general and in the semiconductor industry. A standardization readiness framework would provide a structured approach to help the industry develop a standardization strategy for a specific technology area. The StRL scale would accompany the framework and provide a common language for guiding standardization efforts from prestandards research to adoption, simplifying the understanding and communication of the standards process and lifecycle. During the workshop, NIST presented an outline for a draft framework based on prior efforts for quantum technology. The framework considered the broad elements of technology, market, and community and how these elements converge to help determine a strategy for a new standards activity. Using this draft framework outline as a reference, workshop participants engaged in breakout session discussions exploring whether such a framework could be adapted for semiconductor industry standards activities. The participants agreed with the baseline

established by the draft framework. However, they recommended expanding the framework to include other considerations such as:

- Addressing special factors for critical and emerging technologies in the semiconductor industry;

- Focusing on strategic priorities aligned with technology and standardization roadmaps;

- Ensuring standards efforts address industry problems and needs;

- Incorporating best practices for community-building;

- Promoting industry-wide collaboration, such as using incubators for pre-standardization collaboration; and

- Highlighting the benefits of standardization efforts. Additionally, the framework's alignment with current industry standards initiation practices highlights opportunities to leverage existing frameworks for future collaborative development across the standards ecosystem. During the workshop, NIST also introduced a draft nine-point StRL scale that has a similar layout to existing readiness assessment scales such as the technology readiness level (TRL) scale. The concept of an StRL scale is to provide a common language for tracking standardization efforts with levels ranging from pre-standards research to adoption. There was a broad consensus that a simple-to-use StRL scale would be a valuable tool for the semiconductor industry. The workshop underscored the importance of both tools—the standardization readiness framework and StRL scale—in helping to foster timely and focused standards development that aligns with technological advancements and industry needs. Additionally, these tools could be used to train future standards professionals and to help progress innovative standards concepts from early research to development stages and beyond.

## INTRODUCTION CHIPS FOR AMERICA

The CHIPS and Science Act4 appropriated $50 billion to the Department of Commerce’s CHIPS for America program, both to support semiconductor R&D and to expand semiconductor manufacturing capacity in the United States. This includes $39 billion for the Department of Commerce (the Department) to expand domestic semiconductor manufacturing capacity through the incentives program and $11 billion to advance U.S. leadership in semiconductor R&D. These investments, across both the R&D and incentives programs, seek to strengthen U.S. competitiveness, support domestic manufacturing and innovation, and create good jobs across the country. Within CHIPS for America, the National Institute of Standards and Technology’s (NIST) CHIPS R&D Office (CRDO) has aimed to accelerate the development and commercial deployment of foundational semiconductor technologies by establishing, connecting, and providing access to domestic research efforts, tools, resources, workers, and facilities. A key element to achieving these CHIPS R&D goals is to accelerate the private sector-led development and deployment by industry of effective technical standards.

## BACKGROUND FOR CHIPS R&D STANDARDS EFFORTS

CHIPS R&D has developed a comprehensive standards roadmap in response to calls from the private sector for semiconductor standards efforts, the requirements of CHIPS legislation, and the provisions of the United States Government National Standards Strategy for Critical and Emerging Technologies (USG NSS CET)1 , as summarized below:

- Standards were identified by private sector stakeholders as a core competency for CHIPS R&D. Both the need for standards and ensuring that standards align across different stakeholders were highlighted in many of the responses to NIST’s request for information to guide the design of CHIPS programs5 .

- The CHIPS Act provision (15 USC §4656 (e)) copied below, specifies that private-sector-led technical standards for the semiconductor industry should be an integral part of the CHIPS R&D strategy: “The Director of the National Institute of Standards and Technology shall carry out a microelectronics research program to enable advances and breakthroughs in measurement science, standards, material characterization, instrumentation, testing, and manufacturing capabilities that will accelerate the underlying research and development for metrology of next generation microelectronics and ensure the competitiveness and leadership of the United States within this sector” (emphasis added).

- The CHIPS and Science Act (42 USC §18951(a)) specifies guiding principles for standards, which include: (1) Openness, transparency, due process, balance of interests, appeals, and consensus in the development of international standards are critical; (2) Voluntary consensus standards, developed through an industry-led process, serve as the cornerstone of the United States standardization system and have become the basis of a sound national economy and the key to global market access; (3) Strengthening the unique United States public-private partnerships approach to standards development is critical to United States economic competitiveness; and (4) The United States Government should ensure cooperation and coordination across Federal agencies to partner with and support private sector stakeholders to continue to shape international dialogues in regard to standards development for emerging technologies.

- The United States Government National Standards Strategy for Critical and Emerging Technologies (USG NSS CET) has four major objectives for CETs, including semiconductors and microelectronics: investment, participation, workforce, and integrity and inclusivity.

## CHIPS R&D

Workshops: Introduction and Overview

## CHIPS R&D WORKSHOPS: INTRODUCTION AND OVERVIEW

The purpose of the CHIPS R&D Standardization Readiness Level Workshop6 (from here, ‘workshop’) was to convene thought leaders across the semiconductor industry to discuss and validate the concept of standardization readiness and assess if there is community interest in pursuing the development of two tools:

- A standardization readiness framework that considers the readiness of technology, market, and community to inform a standardization strategy; and

- A standards readiness level scale (StRL), analogous to manufacturing readiness level (MRL) and technology readiness level (TRL)3 scales, that provides a common language for tracking a standard across its lifecycle from concept to adoption. A standardization readiness framework would provide a structured approach to help the industry develop a standardization strategy for a specific technology area. The StRL scale would accompany the framework and provide a common language for guiding standardization efforts from pre-standards research to adoption, simplifying the understanding and communication of the standards process and lifecycle. The workshop aimed to explore whether a framework, with an accompanying StRL scale tailored for use in semiconductor standards development, could help determine whether research outcomes should be integrated into voluntary industry standards activities. The workshop was planned by an organizing committee comprising leaders from standards setting organizations (SSOs), industry, and government representatives (see Appendix A). The workshop was a one-and-a-half-day virtual event. The agenda, found in Appendix B, featured keynote and panel sessions with speakers from across the semiconductor sector. The invited keynote speakers and panelists provided insights in each of the topic areas to stimulate conversations in the subsequent breakout sessions. During the moderated breakout sessions, the workshop participants reflected on the speaker insights and discussed a framework and scale tailored for application to semiconductor-related technology. The approach used to capture input from participants to inform this report is described in Appendix C. The workshop brought together over 170 participants representing a range of stakeholders from the semiconductor industry, including industry consortia and alliances; academia; government entities; and standardssetting organizations (SSOs). This demographic from all critical sectors of the semiconductor ecosystem ensured a comprehensive discussion on the development of a standardization readiness framework and StRL scale. The design of the workshop focused on five topic areas, all working toward the overall goal of gathering perspectives on standardization readiness considerations that help to inform a standardization strategy and how these could be translated to a scale. The key discussion topics included:

- Technology Considerations

- Market Considerations

- Community Considerations

- Standardization Readiness Strategy

- Standards Readiness Level Scale A summary of the key takeaways from these discussions are provided in the following sections.

Standardization Readiness Framework

## STANDARDIZATION READINESS FRAMEWORK

The workshop explored whether a structured, yet flexible standardization readiness framework can be developed for use across the semiconductor standards ecosystem, including by industry leaders, regulatory bodies, and standardization organizations. One of the main goals of a potential framework is to facilitate the timely and appropriate development of standards that align with industry needs and technological evolution. To set the stage for the discussions, panelists were presented with an example notional NIST framework7 , excerpted below, covering the following considerations:

- Technology Considerations: Including technology maturity, TRLs, system context, adjacent and competing technologies, metrology maturity, and existing standards.

- Market Considerations: Including products, supply chain, use cases, market awareness, policy considerations, infrastructure, and intellectual property (IP).

- Community Considerations: Including potential users, stakeholder interest, societal factors, sustainability and environment, and broad-based benefits. Within this framework, the development of a standardization strategy is crucial in providing a structured plan to implement standards development systematically and effectively. As part of developing a standardization strategy, participants were encouraged to consider factors such as:

- what specific aspects of the technology need standardization,

- the type of deliverables the strategy should inform (e.g., international standards, technical specifications, white papers, etc.), and

- the optimal timing for pursuing standardization to be aligned with the pace of technological innovation. Together, a framework would help users assess technology readiness, market conditions, community acceptance, and other factors to inform a strategy to transition pre-standards research into standards activities. The following sections describe key themes from the workshop discussions related to each of these major considerations.

## TECHNOLOGY CONSIDERATIONS

Technology considerations evaluate various factors impacting the state of technological innovations for standardization activities. These considerations help determine whether standardization will support scalability, enhance interoperability, and foster a stable transition from research to commercialization for technological innovations. During the workshop, participants discussed key technology considerations crucial for deciding when to initiate standards development. Key themes discussed by participants included:

- Technology Adoption

- Relationship to Technology Maturity

- Analysis of Existing Standards

- Emerging Technology Considerations

- Standardization Roadmaps

- Focus on Strategic Priorities

Technology Adoption Participants emphasized that understanding the state of technology adoption in the marketplace is critical for developing and effectively implementing standards to support and validate technological advancements. The transition of technologies through stages from pre-standards research to commercialization is vital, requiring an understanding of user needs and addressing vulnerabilities that could impact this progression. Scalability, market demand, and the availability of infrastructure and skilled workers must also be evaluated to ensure successful adoption. Lastly, aligning technology with market trends and swiftly responding to the pace of technological change helps to capitalize on market demand and maintain competitiveness. Relationship to Technology Maturity Understanding the maturity of the technology is an important consideration for determining its readiness for standardization efforts. A key step in this process is assessing technology maturity using tools like the existing Technology Readiness Level Scale (TRL) scale3 . Participants agreed that TRL provides an effective metric for evaluating technology readiness and maturity and should be part of the standardization strategy. Workshop participants also suggested supplementing TRL with additional metrics for a more comprehensive assessment. For example, for technologies that are part of an overall system, participants emphasized applying additional metrics outside of TRL, such as Systems Readiness Level (SRL), to help determine their readiness for standardization activities. Participants also stressed the importance of maintaining global consensus on TRL definitions and metrics for consistency in standardization readiness assessments. Analysis of Existing Standards Participants emphasized that a systematic analysis of existing standards in the technology area is crucial for creating an effective standardization strategy. Participants stressed the need to evaluate current standards activities to identify where standards already exist that can be leveraged or adapted for an emerging need and to identify gaps where standards are needed. Building from existing standards that are already implemented by industry, where applicable, can help reduce duplicate standards efforts and ensure a standard is driven by a market need. Emerging Technology Considerations Participants agreed that the framework should include special considerations for emerging technologies. For example, emerging technologies often need to stabilize and require an assessment of the ‘right time’ to initiate standardization efforts. Another consideration is the state of the broader industry and marketplace for which the emerging technology would play a role. Established sectors often have standards in place before technology is developed and commercialized, whereas in emerging industries technology commercialization is often prioritized over standards development. The potential scalability, longevity, and broad market acceptance are also key factors to consider for standards supporting emerging technologies. Standardization Roadmaps Participants noted that creating a standardization roadmap that complements industry’s technical roadmaps (e.g., the International Roadmap for Devices and Systems8 , Microelectronics and Advanced Packaging Technologies

Roadmap9 , etc.) is critical. The community must align on the needs and requirements before standards supporting a technology can be defined and adopted. The participants noted that successful adoption and use of a standard is the most important metric of success. Focus on Strategic Priorities Aligning standardization efforts with the semiconductor industry’s strategic technical priorities is critical for efficient resource allocation and long-term innovation. One option for identifying strategic priorities is through developing standardization roadmaps aligned with technology roadmaps, as just described above. This alignment also enhances stakeholder buy-in by demonstrating the technical and economic impact of standards.

## MARKET CONSIDERATIONS

Market considerations evaluate various factors influencing the feasibility and impact of establishing industry-wide standards. This includes assessing the business case for standardization, the diversity and maturity of existing markets, the presence of competitive landscapes, consumer confidence, and supply chain robustness. These factors help determine whether standardization will facilitate equitable market access, stimulate innovation, and support sustainable competition and growth in the semiconductor industry. During the workshop, participants were asked what market considerations they believed would be important in helping to decide when to initiate standards development. Key themes discussed by participants included:

- Addressing Market Needs

- Aligning Standards to Industry Needs

- Intellectual Property (IP) Considerations Addressing Market Needs Participants emphasized that having a clear market demand provides a strong business case for investing in or initiating the development of a standard. A strong and robust market (or the potential of an emerging market) can help ensure a return on investment (ROI) for standards development activities and increase adoption following implementation. Participants noted the market’s size, maturity, and competitive landscape are helpful indicators of a standard's market relevance and proposed identifying use cases to help articulate the standard’s feasibility. Another priority was reducing the time it takes to develop and adopt standards to minimize fragmentation and ensure the standard’s relevance. Solving Industry Problems Participants stressed the importance of developing standards that solve specific industry problems for a standard to be "marketable," rather than standards driven by individual companies seeking to promote their own interests. One example participants cited was the evolution of the 300 millimeter (mm) wafer standards. Standards efforts transitioned from traditional, smaller wafer sizes to standards for 300mm wafers in fabrication facilities (fabs). This was due to industry identifying the ‘problem’ of evolving lab technologies and the need for 300mm wafer fab standards to ensure compatibility and interoperability among equipment and processes. Participants noted that describing the problem statement early could facilitate developing standards that enhance interoperability, reduce costs, increase collaboration, and promote innovation.

Intellectual Property Considerations Participants highlighted the challenge of balancing the protection of IP with the need for open and accessible standards. A robust standardization strategy and framework could provide a pathway to create standards that do not necessitate patents while establishing guidelines that protect innovation and still allow the use of some IP. Participants expressed concern that patented technologies could become barriers to effective standardization, particularly in terms of cost to comply with the standard.

## COMMUNITY CONSIDERATIONS

Community considerations evaluate various factors involving actively engaging stakeholders from various sectors—such as manufacturers, suppliers, researchers, and regulatory bodies—to ensure that standards are developed collaboratively. This approach emphasizes consensus building, transparency in decision-making processes, and adaptability to accommodate perspectives and needs from across the semiconductor community. By involving all relevant parties, these strategies aim to create standards that are widely adopted, effective, and capable of supporting innovation and industry growth. During the workshop, participants were asked what community aspects they thought were important when deciding to initiate standards development. Key themes discussed by participants included:

- Community and Stakeholder Engagement Methods

- Standards Education and Workforce Development

- Increasing Collaboration Across the Standards Ecosystem

- International Collaboration

- Standards Education and Workforce Development Community and Stakeholder Engagement Methods Participants emphasized the importance of using various methods, such as conferences and surveys, to engage stakeholders from academia, industry, government, and nonprofits, ensuring balanced representation in standards development. Lowering barriers, like cost and time, by hosting virtual events or offering more education, can enhance community involvement. Early stakeholder engagement and securing buy-in are critical for successful standard adoption. Open-source tools and platforms were highlighted as key to fostering collaboration, reducing entry barriers for smaller organizations, and encouraging a range of contributions. This approach helps build trust, accelerates innovation, and leads to more widely accepted standards. Increasing Collaboration Across the Standards Ecosystem Participants recommended increasing broad collaboration across the semiconductor ecosystem to ensure standards efforts meet market needs and reduce siloed or duplicative standards efforts. Collaboration across entities was seen as key, especially to give small and medium-sized companies a voice in standard creation along with standards implementers. Participants proposed establishing formal partnerships and communication channels among stakeholders, including industry, academia, and regulatory bodies, and by organizing regular collaborative workshops and conferences to align goals and share insights. Additionally, creating unified platforms for standardization efforts and incentivizing joint initiatives can further enhance cooperative engagement. By

encouraging open dialogue and shared objectives, the semiconductor industry can create cohesive standards that reflect market diversity, streamline processes, and enhance its ability to address emerging challenges. International Collaboration Participants emphasized the importance of a comprehensive, globally relevant standardization approach, including working with allies and collecting input from multiple stakeholders. Participants recommended that standardization strategies should encourage international collaboration, which would promote the development of interoperable, non-competing standards. Such global cooperation would make the standardization process more strategic, efficient, and cost-effective for the industry. Standards Incubators for Emerging Technology Standards incubators bring together the community to develop and nurture standards ideas for emerging technology areas. Additionally, standards incubators could provide supportive environments with mentoring, training, and convening resources to facilitate standards development2 . Participants highlighted the use of standards incubators as a best practice for semiconductor standards. Participants also noted the opportunity for the community to develop more pre-competitive incubators for emerging technologies, as the options are currently limited. Standards Education and Workforce Development Standards education and workforce development are crucial for ensuring a skilled workforce capable of understanding, creating, and implementing standards. Participants highlighted the need for collaboration between companies and educational institutions to support ongoing investment in standards education. Proposed methods for encouraging participation and continued education in standards development included providing educational incentives and credits, delivering ongoing professional training, and celebrating and rewarding contributions to standards development.

## CONSIDERATIONS FOR A STANDARDIZATION STRATEGY

A standardization strategy outlines the approach for developing and implementing standards supporting specific technologies, ensuring they meet industry needs and align with broader market and community considerations. It should be informed by a framework that considers aspects such as what to standardize, the type of deliverables required, and the timing for standardization efforts. The strategy should also consider the capacity, commitment, and representation of stakeholders across the technology, market, and community to ensure the successful development and adoption of the standards. During the workshop, participants were asked to explore the challenge of developing a standardization strategy informed by a framework and were tasked with identifying key considerations for a standardization strategy. Key themes identified by the participants were:

- Clear Standardization Processes

- Timeliness of Standards Development

Clear Standardization Process Establishing a standardization strategy requires consistent processes for standardization to minimize costs, maximize efficiency, and reduce time-to-market. Clear steps, exit criteria, and metrics must guide the progression from ideation to implementation. Participants emphasized the importance of incorporating testing and security elements into the standards earlier in the process, which is often overlooked. Defining the scope and limitations of the standardization effort is essential for creating a plan that addresses when to act (now, near term, or long term), the form the standard should take (international standards, code, recommendation, etc.), and key considerations (requirements, consensus, technology maturity, etc.). This structured approach ensures a thorough and efficient path to standardization. Timeliness of Standards Development Participants emphasized the importance of starting standardization efforts early in product development to align with industry requirements and avoid stifling innovation or causing fragmentation. Clear definitions and requirements should be established to prioritize what to standardize and to optimize timing. They also emphasized the importance of evaluating both the urgency of the problem and stakeholder commitment to ensure that the process remains timely and effective.

## ADDITIONAL FRAMEWORK RECOMMENDATIONS

The workshop discussions also led to recommendations regarding additional considerations for a standardization readiness framework that were outside of the topics just described. Some of those key recommendations included:

- Highlight Benefits of Standardization Participants emphasized the need for a framework that balances the costs and benefits of standardization efforts, helping stakeholders assess feasibility and ensure a return on investment (ROI). A thorough costbenefit analysis is essential to ensure the financial returns justify the investment.

- Framework Flexibility and Adaptability Participants stressed the importance of a flexible and adaptable framework for standardization to accommodate different scenarios, processes, and users. The framework should be easily updated to reflect ongoing developments and evolving processes. For example, the framework should be adaptable to new trends such as the use of performance-based standards, which might be better suited to the rapid pace of innovation than prescriptive standards. Participants also recommended that the framework could include tools such as questionnaires, flowcharts, and matrices to help determine which technology components to standardize and in what order.

- Leverage Existing Frameworks Participants noted that many existing SSOs have procedures for initiating new standards activities. The proposed standardization readiness framework's alignment with current industry standards initiation practices highlights opportunities for future collaborative development across the standards ecosystem.

Standardization Readiness Level Scale

## STANDARDIZATION READINESS LEVEL SCALE

Along with discussing considerations for developing a standardization readiness framework, workshop participants shared their insights on considerations for creating a standards readiness level (StRL) scale to inform the standardization process. The proposed StRL scale, like the TRL scale for technology readiness, aims to provide a common language for tracking the development of standards from initial concept to full adoption. The use of the scale could also help to justify committing resources for standardization initiatives. A notional nine-point StRL scale for informing the standardization process was presented to workshop participants. The example scale was divided into three broad sections to facilitate a discussion:

- Readiness to Standardize

- Standard Development

- Readiness of the Standard During the workshop discussions, participants emphasized several key factors to consider when developing an StRL scale including:

- Key Milestones in the Standardization Process

- Notional Scale Levels

- Learning from Other Scales

## KEY MILESTONES IN THE STANDARDIZATION PROCESS

Participants emphasized the need for a clearly defined StRL scale with levels based on key milestones in the standardization process. The scale should account for the full complexity of standardization, from assessing readiness for development to implementation. It should include exit criteria, adoption milestones, and readiness metrics that can be adjusted for future use. Participants emphasized the importance of defining precise metrics to gauge adoption rates, especially as competition in the standardization landscape grows more intense. Participants also highlighted the need to clarify the scale points and their application by end users to effectively address each level in the process of creating a new standard.

## NOTIONAL SCALE LEVELS

Participants agreed with the three broad sections of the notional scale that was presented. They expounded that the early stages of the StRL scale (notional levels one through three) should focus on pre-standardization activities such as research, problem definition, market influence, and community involvement. These stages should assess the readiness of technologies and ensure high confidence in the standard's success and should inform a standards development strategy. The standards development stages (notional levels four through six) should address metrology, testing, and consensus building, with a focus on interoperability and capturing necessary information. The final stages (notional levels seven through nine) should ensure the implementation, evaluation, and revision of the standard, with a focus on increasing adoption and meeting community needs.

## LEARNING FROM OTHER SCALES

There are several readiness level scales currently used across industries, including Integration Readiness Level (IRL), SRL, MRL, and TRL. TRL was the first scale developed to assess the maturity of technology and remains widely used. By analogy, StRL focuses on the readiness of a standard, considering technology readiness, market readiness and community acceptance, among other factors. Some preliminary drafts of the StRL scale aimed to align its levels with those of the existing TRL scale. Workshop participants agreed that while TRL and StRL should be related in terms of their number of levels and appearance, they must remain distinct, with TRL serving as a potential tool within an overall StRL framework. There was no consensus on how to correlate TRL with StRL. However, participants emphasized the need for an adaptable StRL framework and scale that accommodates the unique requirements of different sectors, such as hardware and software.

Summary and Next Steps

## SUMMARY AND NEXT STEPS WORKSHOP SUMMARY

The CHIPS R&D Standardization Readiness Level Workshop brought together over 170 stakeholders and technical experts to discuss metrics for a standardization readiness framework and standards readiness level scale tailored for use in the semiconductor industry. To inspire discussion, an existing, notional NIST standardization readiness framework was presented—with elements of technology, market, and community. Participants agreed that the notional framework provided a good baseline of considerations that are also applicable to semiconductor standards development. Additionally, participants recommended that the notional NIST framework should be expanded to include considerations such as addressing special factors for critical and emerging technologies, focusing on strategic priorities aligned with standardization roadmaps, ensuring standards address industry problems and needs, and incorporating best practices for community-building, such as highlighting the role of incubators in providing opportunities to collaborate on pre-standardization activities. Overall, they agreed that a consensus framework would help inform standards strategies and should be further developed. Participants agreed that a simple-to-use standards readiness level scale would be useful for understanding and communicating the standards process and lifecycle from concept to adoption. Participants recommended that the StRL scale should be a nine-or ten-point scale to align with the norms of other similar readiness level scales such as TRL or MRL, with the number of levels dependent on the outcomes of the actual scale development and deployment. They recognized that the StRL scale would be beneficial for stakeholders to assess readiness at various stages, including the readiness to standardize, the development of standards, and the readiness of those standards. The standardization readiness framework and standards readiness level scale are designed to align standards development with industry needs, promoting innovation and collaboration within the semiconductor sector.

## NEXT STEPS

The next steps should involve refining the standardization readiness framework and standards readiness level scale based on the feedback gathered during the workshop. This includes further defining the type of tool the framework should be and ensuring the framework's and scale’s flexibility to adapt to different standardization scenarios. It also includes defining the criteria and milestones for each level of the StRL scale. Additionally, ongoing engagement with stakeholders across the semiconductor ecosystem will be crucial to validate and implement these tools effectively.

## APPENDIX C: WORKSHOP DATA COLLECTION

During both days of the CHIPS R&D StRL Workshop, moderators, technical facilitators, and note-takers collected data from panelists and breakout session participants to capture their ideas and inputs on both the session topics and discussion questions. These ideas and inputs related to defining and outlining an StRL framework and scale, providing context for their applications, and sharing additional considerations for standardization readiness activities. Given the length of the workshop, the moderators and technical facilitators used multiple knowledge capture methods, including session recordings, meeting minutes, virtual meeting platform chat entries, technical notes, poll responses, and form submissions to ensure all contributions were documented. Reviewing all data sources allowed the report writers to characterize the discussions that took place over the two days of the event and identify key thematic topics.
